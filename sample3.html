Pattern: When platforms turn usage data into personal memories, users feel more attached, share more, and come back more often.

Opportunity

Combine group travel data with the retro/wrap-up trend — turn Airbnb trips into shareable memories that bring travelers back together

Concept

What could we build? What should we build?

Explore

Worked with XFN partners (PM, Eng, Data Science) to brainstorm how Airbnb could turn trip history into a memory layer

A. Wrap-Up Story

A shareable year-in-review showing where you went with your friends — trips, destinations, and travel companions in one story format.

B. Retro by Destination

A destination-first view — where I went and who was with me. Each city shows the people you shared that trip with.

C. Retro by People

A people-first view — who I traveled with and where we've been. Each person shows shared trips and destinations.

Analyze

Evaluated each direction with XFN stakeholders across scope, user value, and engineering cost

A. Wrap-Up Story B. By Destination C. By People
Scope Large — custom story engine, animations, sharing infra Medium — extends existing Past Trips data Small — leverages booking data + guest profiles
Pros High virality, shareable Strong travel memory, links places + people Drives rebooking, relationship-first, aligns with retention data
Cons Seasonal (once/year), high eng cost Doesn't directly drive social action Needs privacy guardrails
Eng Cost High Medium Low–Medium
Verdict Explored Explored Chosen

XFN alignment: Option C best serves the retention thesis — people-first memory directly enables "rebook with" flows and has the lowest eng cost to ship.

What I Learned

Key takeaways from designing Connections

  • Taxonomy is product design — how you categorize Experiences shapes what users can discover; occasion-first unlocked hidden inventory
  • Start curated, then automate — hand-picked Collections built trust; ML-powered Categories scaled it to millions of listings
  • Design for groups, not just individuals — 52% travel with others, but most flows assume a solo booker
  • Privacy drives adoption — opt-in defaults and full user control meant zero backlash at launch
  • Compound your features — Collections → Categories → Social — each layer built on the last, creating a moat
